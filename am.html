01 / FIRST THINGGetting there — and back

You can drop your bags at the apartment on arrival — you just can't take possession (the keys, the flat itself) until 15:00. That changes Friday for the better: no lockers, no dragging suitcases around, hands free from the moment you leave the flat again.

Pay with a contactless card. The Netherlands runs OVpay — tap your credit card or phone directly on the reader, on NS trains and GVB trams and buses alike. No ticket machine, no OV-chipkaart. Each person needs their own card. Tap in and tap out, or you're charged the maximum fare. More on how you'll actually get around once you're here, in section 04.

Friday 31 July — landing 11:00

  • Out of Schiphol by roughly 12:15. Sprinter to Station Sloterdijk (~11 min), then bus 15 to Mercatorplein (~10 min), then a 6-minute walk — drop the bags and get the keys, ahead of the official 15:00 check-in. Roughly €6.70pp, 30–35 minutes door to door. You're in, hands empty, keys in pocket, by about 13:00.
  • Straight out again to De Wallen / the medieval core — tram 7 or bus 18 from Jan Evertsenstraat, ~20 min. No need to rush back for anything; see the Friday block below for how much room that actually gives you.

Tuesday 4 August — flight 12:00

Long-haul, non-Schengen: be at Schiphol by 09:15. Leave the flat at 08:00 — bus 15 from Mercatorplein to Station Sloterdijk (~10 min), then the Sprinter to Schiphol (11 min, every few minutes). At the terminal by ~08:55. There's a full hour of buffer in that plan. No cooking, no mess to clean the night before — see Sunday's plan below for why that's already handled.

What you should actually enjoy

The Homomonument, Westermarkt. Three pink granite triangles set into the pavement, unveiled 1987 — the first monument anywhere to commemorate the persecution of gay people. It sits directly on your Sunday canal walk. Free, fifteen minutes, and it's the part of the fortnight that means something rather than performs something.

The Canal Parade, if you want it. Boats depart 12:00 from the east and reach the Westerdok finish 15:00–18:00 — a three-hour spread, so pick your stretch. Nieuwe Herengracht, 12:00–13:15, is the thinnest crowd, right at the start. But this is Haarlem's day, and that trade still favours Haarlem.

Missed by a day and a halfThe free Open Air Film Festival is on your own square, Mercatorplein, 29–30 July. You land the 31st.

02Where you actually are

Vasco da Gamastraat sits at the southern edge of De Baarsjes, by the Postjeswetering canal. This is planned 1920s Amsterdam, built in the Amsterdam School brick style — low, residential, genuinely mixed: Moroccan and Turkish shops that have been here forty years, next to third-wave coffee bars that arrived in the last ten.

03Getting around — and the bike question

Trams, buses and the metro all run on OVpay — tap in, tap out, one card per person. A single ride is a flat fare (~€1); if you're making several trips a day, a GVB day ticket (~€9, unlimited trams/buses/metro/ferries) is worth doing the maths on at a machine or in the app.

Tram works — check 9292.nl every time, not the week before

The Oranje Loper rebuild has Rozengracht dug up, with disruption running into 2027. Tram 17 is cut short at Surinameplein from 4 July to 19 August 2026 — a shuttle tram 27 covers the rest. Trams 13 and 18, and bus 18, use the same corridor and may also be diverted. Bus 15 from Mercatorplein to Sloterdijk is nowhere near any of it — that's why it's your airport route throughout this guide. For anything else, check 9292.nl or the Google/Apple Maps transit layer on the day.

Ferries across the IJ to Amsterdam Noord are free, frequent (every 15–20 min), and take bikes and pedestrians alike — this is how you'd reach NDSM Werf, see Day trips.

Bikes — parked for now, not off the table

Nothing in this guide assumes you're cycling. If the mood strikes later in the trip, Fietspadje (700 m, ~€10/day) is still in the full directory below — walk in, no booking needed, and De Baarsjes is about as gentle a place to try it as exists in this city: flat, quiet, four minutes from a park. No pressure either way.

07Organising the Rijksmuseum visit

Your slot is booked: Monday, 09:00, staying until lunchtime. A few things worth knowing before you go, gathered from people who go often.

  • Arrive 10 minutes early. There's a brief bag check at the door — anything larger than an A4 sheet goes into the free cloakroom, so don't bring a day pack you're attached to.
  • Go straight up to the second floor — the Gallery of Honour — and skip the early-medieval rooms near the entrance for now. That's where the crowds build first later in the morning; at 09:00 you can stand nearly alone in front of the Vermeers.
  • Route: Gallery of Honour → the Night Watch at its end → the surrounding 17th-century Golden Age rooms. That's the two-to-three-hour visit most people actually have time for, and it comfortably fits "until lunchtime."
  • No exit time limit — your ticket only fixes your entry, so once you're in you can stay as long as you like, right up to close if the morning runs long.
  • Photography is fine in the permanent galleries — no flash, no tripod.
  • The free Rijksmuseum app has an offline audio tour with about 14 highlight stops, worth downloading over the flat's wifi the night before.
  • If you want more after the Golden Age rooms and still have time: the Asian Pavilion and the gardens are usually near-empty even when the second floor is packed — a good pressure valve if one of you wants a slower half hour.
  • Getting there Monday morning: trams 2 and 12 stop at "Rijksmuseum" from the Centraal side, but from De Baarsjes the route depends on what's running around the Oranje Loper works — check 9292.nl that morning rather than assume a line number. On foot it's about 34 minutes through Rembrandtpark and Vondelpark, entirely pleasant if the morning is dry and you'd rather not deal with transit at all.

08Day trips

Haarlem & Zandvoort — Saturday, all day

Covered in full in Saturday's block above. Bus 15 → Sloterdijk → train, ~15 minutes each way, and Zandvoort is a further stop on the same line rather than a second trip. Supper's back in De Baarsjes, not in Haarlem — one less reservation to think about. Fishbar Monk and PRAKM are still there if the day runs differently than planned and staying for dinner ends up appealing.

NDSM Werf — an option, not a plan

Free ferry from behind Centraal — look for F4 or ferry 906, marked NDSM-Werf, departing every 15–20 minutes, roughly a 13-minute crossing. It takes bikes and pedestrians, runs until close to midnight, and costs nothing.

What's there: a former shipyard turned street-art quarter — STRAAT Museum (graffiti and street art in an 8,000 m² warehouse, ~€18–21 per adult, book online in peak season), plus a run of repurposed-container bars and restaurants — Pllek, IJver, Noorderlicht — good for a coffee or a sundowner rather than a full meal.

Things to weigh: it's genuinely across the city from De Baarsjes — allow half a day round trip if you include STRAAT, or a couple of hours if you're just there for the walk and a drink. It's an open industrial site with little shelter, so it's a fair-weather idea, not a rainy-day one. And it competes directly with your open Monday afternoon — there's no obligation to use that slot for this rather than just resting.

10What to actually eat

Fish and seafood

You're here at the right moment — Zeeland mussel season runs mid-July to April, so you land at the very start of the new harvest.

DishForWhat it is
KibbelingYOUCod chunks in light batter, deep-fried. Mild, not "fishy". Zeeduivel does the best near you (closed Sun+Mon).
Zeeuwse mosselenHERIn-season Zeeland mussels — your Sunday-night cook-in, or Mossel & Gin in Westerpark.

On menus, the white fish you can eat: kabeljauw (cod), tong (Dover sole), heilbot (halibut), zeeduivel (monkfish).

The rest of the Dutch canon

  • Bitterballen — crumbed, fried beef ragout with sharp mustard, standing at a bar with a beer. Café Thuys or 't Binnepretje.
  • Frites — thick-cut, twice-fried. Try patatje oorlog — peanut sauce, mayo, raw onion.
  • Stroopwafel — warm, pressed to order, from a market stall. Not the packaged kind.
  • Appeltaart — dense, cinnamon, unsweetened whipped cream. Winkel 43, right on your Sunday route.
  • Oude kaas — aged Gouda, crystalline and salty. De Kaashut will let you taste before you buy.
  • Jenever — the juniper spirit gin descends from, served brim-full in a tulip glass. A. van Wees Proeflokaal does tasting flights.

The two cuisines that are actually Amsterdam's

Indonesian and Surinamese — colonial legacies, eaten constantly by Amsterdammers, and closer to you than the postcard food ever gets. De Indo van Jan Eef and Rotishop Gieta are both under 350 m from the door; Toko Bersama West (4.9 stars, 11,500+ reviews) is the best of the lot.

12Practical

  • Trams: see Getting Around above — Oranje Loper works affect trams 13/17/18 into 2027. Bus 15 (your airport route) is unaffected.
  • Cafés close at 17:00. No evening coffee culture here. Bo's Amsterdam (Thu–Sat to 22:00) is the exception.
  • Sundays shut the greengrocers, most bakeries, Ten Katemarkt, Zeeduivel, De Kaashut. AH and Aldi on Postjesweg stay open.
  • Mondays shut a surprising number of restaurants — see the closed-Monday box in Monday's block.
  • You're a pedestrian in a city where the bicycle has right of way. Red brick = bike lane, not pavement.
  • Noise: hardest to talk in are Bar Baarsch, Foodhallen, Mossel & Gin, Fishbar Monk. Easiest: Abbey, Café Thuys, Noi, any terrace.
  • Cash: most places take cards, but some Turkish/Surinamese spots and bike deposits are cash-only. Carry ~€100 in notes.
  • Night shop: Avondwinkel de Buren on Postjesweg (4 min, to 02:00) — the honest one. Avoid the one on Jan Evertsenstraat.
  • Weather, early August: 20–24°C, rain at some point. A light waterproof beats an umbrella — the wind will win.
