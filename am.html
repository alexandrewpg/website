02Schiphol, both ways

Pay with a contactless card. The Netherlands runs OVpay: tap your credit card or phone directly on the reader, on NS trains and on GVB trams and buses alike. No ticket machine, no OV-chipkaart. Each person needs their own card — you cannot tap twice with one. Tap in and tap out, or you're charged the maximum fare.

03Where you actually are

Marco Polostraat sits in De Baarsjes, on the edge of Bos en Lommer. This is planned 1920s Amsterdam: brick, low, residential, built in the Amsterdam School style. No canal boats, no queues. One of the genuinely mixed neighbourhoods in the city — Moroccan and Turkish shops that have been here forty years, next to third-wave coffee bars that arrived in the last ten.

05What to actually eat

Fish and seafood

You are here at the right moment. The Zeeland mussel season runs mid-July to April, so you arrive at the very start of the new harvest — the best shellfish moment of the Dutch year. The 2026 Hollandse Nieuwe herring season opened 16 June.

DishForWhat it is
KibbelingYOUCod chunks in light batter, deep-fried, with garlic or remoulade sauce. Mild, hot, not "fishy". Every market has a stall. Zeeduivel does the best near you.
Zeeuwse mosselenHERZeeland mussels, in-season, served as a pan on the table with fries and sauces. Mossel & Gin, Westerpark.
Hollandse garnalenHERTiny North Sea brown shrimp, hand-peeled, intensely sweet. Best as a garnalenkroket — one of the great Dutch things.
Zeeuwse oestersHERWorld-class. In August expect Creuse rock oysters; the prized flat oysters are a Sept–April thing.
HaringHERRaw brined herring with onion and pickle, from a street cart, ~€4. The national dish. Oily and strongly marine — not for you.
Gerookte palingHERSmoked eel. Beloved, and very strong. Not for you.
On menus, the white fish you can eat: kabeljauw (cod), tong (Dover sole), heilbot (halibut), zeeduivel (monkfish — literally "sea devil").

The cheapest way to solve the seafood problem

Zeeduivel Visspecialiteiten — 1.3 km, 6 min by bike. A fish counter, not a restaurant, 4.9 stars. You get kibbeling; she gets oysters, gamba's, tuna tataki, herring. The two of you eat brilliantly for about €30 and nobody compromises. Do it as a lunch. Closed Sunday and Monday.

The rest of the Dutch canon

  • Bitterballen. Crumbed, deep-fried balls of beef ragout, eaten with sharp mustard, standing at a bar with a beer. This is the centre of Dutch borrel culture and you should do it at least twice. Any brown café; Café 't Binnepretje or Café Thuys.
  • Frites. Thick-cut, twice-fried, in a paper cone. Order them with mayo (correct), or patatje oorlog — "war fries", buried under peanut sauce, mayonnaise, and raw onion. Freddy Fryday West or Frites & Frites.
  • Broodje kroket / uitsmijter. Lunch, Dutch-style: a croquette split open on a soft roll with mustard, or three fried eggs on bread with ham and cheese. Every café does them.
  • Stroopwafel. Not the packaged kind. Buy it warm, pressed to order, from a market stall — Ten Katemarkt or Lindengracht. Completely different object.
  • Appeltaart. Dense, tall, cinnamon-and-raisin, served with unsweetened whipped cream. Winkel 43 at the Noordermarkt is the famous one and it's a two-minute walk from the Saturday Lindengracht market. Back to Black does an enormous one closer to home.
  • Oude kaas. Aged Gouda — crystalline, salty, nothing like the young stuff. De Kaashut (1 km) will let you taste before you buy. Get a wedge for the flat.
  • Hagelslag. Chocolate sprinkles on buttered bread, for breakfast, by adults, without irony. Buy a box at Dirk. Do it once.
  • Jenever. The juniper spirit that gin descends from — maltier, rounder, served in a tulip glass filled to the brim, so the first sip is taken with hands behind your back. A kopstootje ("little headbutt") is a jenever with a beer chaser. A. van Wees Proeflokaal (Herengracht 319) does tasting flights and is not expensive.

The two cuisines that are actually Amsterdam's

Indonesian and Surinamese — both legacies of Dutch colonialism, both eaten constantly by Amsterdammers, and both far more characteristic of this city than pancakes. You have outstanding examples of each within 400 metres:

  • De Indo van Jan Eef (110 m) — build-your-own Indonesian rice plate, takeaway. Closed Monday.
  • Rotishop Gieta (320 m) — Surinamese roti: a soft flatbread with curried chicken or lamb, potato, long beans, and a yellow chilli sauce that is genuinely hot. €. Closed Monday.
  • Toko Bersama West (1.6 km) — 4.9 stars over 11,500 reviews. Ask for Rames Complete. Probably the best cheap meal you'll eat.

A full rijsttafel — a dozen small Indonesian dishes around rice — is the classic special-occasion meal, but it's a €50-a-head affair. The counter versions above give you 80% of it for a quarter of the price.

06Bikes

Is it intimidating? Yes — for about ninety minutes. Then it isn't.

What's genuinely hard: the traffic is dense and fast, everyone assumes you know the rules, Dutch cyclists ride at 20 km/h and will ring rather than brake, and scooters share the lane. Nobody is being aggressive; they're commuting, and you're in the way.

What's easier than you fear: it's flat, the infrastructure is superb, and De Baarsjes is calm. You are not learning to ride in the centre — you're learning on a quiet residential grid, four minutes from Rembrandtpark.

  • Ride right, overtake left. Never stop in the lane — pull off it.
  • Signal with your arm. People actually do, and expect it of you.
  • Trams always have priority. Cross tram tracks at 90° — a wheel in the groove will put you down.
  • Many Dutch rentals have a coaster brake: you brake by pedalling backwards. Ask which you're getting. This is the single most disorienting thing for a North American.
  • Essentially nobody wears a helmet and none will be offered. That's a real choice, not a formality.

Locking: two locks, every single time

Yes, you lock it on the street — there is no alternative and it's what everyone does. But bike theft here is endemic. (1) The ring lock built into the rear wheel. That alone is not enough. (2) A chain or D-lock securing the frame to something fixed — a rack, a post, a railing. Check the second lock is included before you leave the shop.

07Museums — two, not five

Museum Het Schip — the small one

3.3 km, 14 min by bike. Wed–Sun 11:00–17:00. Closed Monday and Tuesday. ~€18.

The masterpiece of the Amsterdam School — the expressionist brick movement that built social housing for workers in the 1910s and 20s. This matters because of where you're staying: Mercatorplein and your entire street are products of the same thinking. You'll walk out and read your own neighbourhood differently. It's a restored workers' flat and an old post office inside a housing block. Small, quiet, well-run. Take the English guided tour — reviewers are unanimous that it's what makes the visit. Check the time and book.

Rijksmuseum — the big one, if you want it

3.2 km, 14 min by bike. Daily 09:00–17:00. €25 per adult. Online only — there are no door sales.

Book the 09:00 slot on Monday 3 August. The weekend is WorldPride; the first hour on a Monday is the emptiest that building gets.

You were right that an immense museum forces a choice. Go straight up to the Gallery of Honour and the 17th century, and ignore everything else. Vermeer, Hals, De Hooch, ending at the Night Watch. Ninety focused minutes beats four exhausted hours. Then leave, get on the bikes, and have lunch somewhere real.

Skip the Van Gogh Museum this trip. It's the most crowded room-for-room museum in the country in a normal week, and this is not one.

And an honest alternative: if you do Haarlem and visit Teylers, you may find you don't want the Rijksmuseum at all. That's a legitimate outcome, and it saves you €50 and a morning.

08Day trips

Haarlem — 15 minutes. Do this one, on Saturday.

From Station Sloterdijk, not Centraal — which is already your transfer station. You are closer to Haarlem than most people staying in Amsterdam. ~€4.50 each way per person, ~15 min.

Why Saturday: the Grote Markt market runs Mondays and Saturdays, 09:00–17:00, and Saturday is the big one — fresh fish, cheese, bread, flowers, produce, under the St. Bavo church, ringed by terraces. Exactly the market-and-walkable-city day you're after. And it takes you cleanly out of Amsterdam on Canal Parade day.

  • 09:45 — arrive. Grote Markt market until midday. Eat from the stalls.
  • Teylers Museum, Spaarne 16. Daily 10:00–17:00, ~€18. The oldest museum in the Netherlands — fossils, minerals, 18th-century scientific instruments, art, and the founder's own house, preserved as a time capsule. Reviewers consistently note it isn't crowded. This may honestly be the better small museum.
  • Or instead: Frans Hals Museum (closed Mondays; Sat 11:00–17:00) — Hals's group portraits, and a serious argument for skipping the Rijksmuseum.
  • Dinner: Fishbar Monk, Turfmarkt 20 (Sat from 12:00) does monkfish and turbot properly alongside oysters and scallops — the one place where you both eat brilliantly off the same menu. Noisy. Or Vis Café Ed on the Grote Markt itself: smaller, oysters and Dover sole. Reserve either. For something cheaper and entirely Dutch: Restaurant PRAKM, Kleine Houtstraat 105 (Wed–Sat 17:00–21:00), 4.9 stars, stamppot and mussel stew, family-run.

COST FOR TWO: ~€18 trains + ~€36 Teylers + food.

Zandvoort aan Zee — 30 minutes. Bolt it onto Haarlem.

Zandvoort is one stop past Haarlem on the same line, so it isn't really a separate trip — it's an afternoon. North Sea beach, dunes, a long row of beach pavilions where you can drink and eat with your feet more or less in the sand. In early August the water is around 18–19 °C, which is swimmable if you're Canadian about it.

The clean version of the day: Haarlem market in the morning → train on to Zandvoort around 14:00 → beach and a pavilion beer → train back to Haarlem for dinner → home by 22:00. Adds about €10 for two.

Caveat: a hot Saturday in August will put half of Amsterdam on that beach. If the forecast is 26 °C and sunny, go Monday instead, or accept the crowd.

Antwerp — 1h25 each way. A real trip, and a real cost.

Here's the thing that makes it more feasible than it looks: the Eurocity Direct to Antwerp leaves from Amsterdam Zuid, not Centraal — and bus 15 from Mercatorplein runs straight to Station Zuid. It's hourly, takes 1h23, and lands you in Antwerpen-Centraal, which is itself one of the great railway buildings of Europe and worth the ticket on its own.

What Antwerp gives you that Amsterdam doesn't: Belgian beer culture (which is a different and deeper thing than Dutch), proper frites, chocolate, a magnificent Gothic cathedral full of Rubens, the MAS museum, and a compact, walkable, food-forward old city with far fewer tourists per square metre.

What it costs you: roughly €60–80 per person return if you book the Eurocity Direct in advance (Eurostar is faster by three minutes and much dearer — don't). Call it €130–160 for the two of you, plus a full day. On a four-day trip, that is a quarter of your time and a fifth of your entire budget.

My honest read

Antwerp is a genuinely wonderful city and it is the wrong call on this particular trip. You have four days, one of which is a write-off (arrival), one of which is claimed by the parade, and you have said you want to feel Amsterdam life and stay away from expensive things. Antwerp costs €150 and buys you eleven hours on trains and in a city you'll only skim.

Haarlem costs €18 and fifteen minutes. Save Antwerp for a trip where it can have two nights.

Realistic central estimate: €750–800 for the two of you, roughly CAD $1,150–1,250.

The three things that move this most: the Saturday dinner in Haarlem (€60 at PRAKM vs €140 at Fishbar Monk); whether you do the Rijksmuseum at all (€50); and coffee, which at Friedhats and DAK prices runs past €100 for the two of you across five days before you buy a single bag of beans. Which, given the neighbourhood you've landed in, you probably should.

11Practical

  • Cafés close at 17:00. There is no evening coffee culture here. Bo's Amsterdam (Thu–Sat to 22:00) is the exception.
  • Sundays shut the greengrocers, most bakeries, Ten Katemarkt, Zeeduivel and the bike shop. Mondays shut a startling number of restaurants. Check the day-blocks above.
  • You are a pedestrian in a city where the bicycle has right of way and the cyclist is unsentimental about it. Red-brick surface = bike lane, not pavement. Look right before you step off a kerb.
  • Noise. Dutch restaurants are hard-surfaced and loud. Worst on this list: Bar Baarsch, Foodhallen, Mossel & Gin, Fishbar Monk. Easiest for conversation: Abbey, Café Thuys, Noi, and any terrace.
  • Cash. Almost everywhere takes cards, but some Turkish and Surinamese places and market stalls are cash-only, and bike deposits usually are. Carry €150 in notes.
  • Language. Everyone speaks English, fluently, and will switch before you finish your first Dutch word. Don't take it personally.
  • Weather, early August: 20–24 °C and it will rain at some point. Bring a light waterproof, not an umbrella — the wind will destroy it.
  • Avoid Mercator Avondverkoop (the night shop on Jan Evertsenstraat). Open till 02:00, and a genuine price trap.
